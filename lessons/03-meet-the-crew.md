# Module 3 — Meet the BMAD Team

> **Where this fits:** This is the fun one — you meet your AI crew, they introduce
> themselves, and you draft your dream team for real jobs. You need nothing before it.
> **The point:** by the end you'll know who's on the BMAD team, what each one is *for*,
> and how to pick the right teammate (or the whole room) for a job.

*Source: SOURCE.md §3 (the agents + Party Mode). Instructor: ROLE-PLAY the intros in
character, one or two at a time — keep it playful and short. Do before tell.*

---

## Do This First

Before anyone introduces themselves, a quick draft pick. Ask (personalize to their build
in `MY_BUILD.md`):

> "You're about to build [their build]. If you could hire **one** specialist to help
> first — not a generic 'AI assistant,' a *specialist* — what job would you want them to
> do? Gut answer, don't overthink it."

Let them answer. Whatever they say, there's a teammate for that. Now let's meet them.

## What Just Happened

You just felt the core idea of this module: **you don't want one AI doing everything —
you want the right specialist for the job.** A dedicated specialist (even an AI one) beats
one AI wearing every hat at once. BMAD ships you an actual *team* of them. Introductions:

## The Idea — Meet the Crew (role-play these, 1–2 at a time, in character)

Walk them in like teammates at a kickoff. A couple of lines each — name, vibe, what they
do, when you'd call them. Suggested character beats (keep it light):

- **Mary — the Analyst.** *"I ask the annoying questions before you waste a week."* Digs
  into the problem, the market, the customer. **Call her when:** the idea is still fuzzy.
- **John — the Product Manager.** *"I write down what we're actually building, and why."*
  Owns the PRD. **Call him when:** it's time to turn an idea into a plan. *(PRD = the
  "what & why" document — define it on first use.)*
- **Winston — the Architect.** *"Decisions, people. Let's not build three login systems."*
  Makes the technical calls explicit. **Call him when:** you need to decide *how* to build.
- **Sally — the UX Designer.** *"How will a human actually use this?"* Owns the experience
  and the interface. **Call her when:** the thing has screens or flows people touch.
- **Amelia — the Developer.** *"Hand me a clear story and I'll build it."* Writes the
  code, one story at a time. **Call her when:** it's time to actually make the thing.
- **Paige — the Tech Writer.** *"Future-you will thank present-you for docs."* Captures
  knowledge so it isn't trapped in one chat. **Call her when:** things need explaining.

And two who work mostly during the *build* (Phase 4 — Implementation): a **Scrum Master**
who keeps the stories flowing, and a **QA / Test** role who makes sure it actually works.
*(BMAD leaves their persona names looser — the role is what matters; you'll see exactly
where they sit in Module 6.)*

> Define on first use: **"agent"** = a specialized AI persona with a role, a focus, and a
> way of talking. Not a different model — a different *perspective*.

> **Big caveat — the lineup changes.** This roster isn't fixed. Which teammates you have
> depends on which **modules** you've installed, and names/personas can shift across BMAD
> versions and modules. So don't memorize the cast — **learn the roles.** "Who decides the
> *how*?" matters more than "is the Architect still called Winston?"

## Party Mode — the whole room at once

Sometimes you don't want one teammate — you want them arguing it out. **Party Mode**
(`bmad-party-mode`) puts the whole crew in one chat. It picks the relevant installed
agents per message; they respond **in character**, agree, disagree, and build on each
other — a real back-and-forth, not a round-robin summary. **Great for:** big decisions
with tradeoffs, brainstorming, post-mortems, and retrospectives.

> Gotcha: Party Mode only brings in the agents you've actually **installed** — an empty
> room can't debate. And most questions don't need a party; reach for it when a call has
> several legitimate perspectives that need to clash.

## Pattern / Anti-pattern

- ✅ **Pattern:** match the teammate to the job — fuzzy idea → Mary; the *how* → Winston;
  a big messy call → the whole room in Party Mode.
- ❌ **Anti-pattern:** asking one generic AI to "be the analyst, PM, architect, and dev
  all at once" in a single breath — the everything-blur that makes AI builds go sideways.
- **How to tell:** if no single role owns the question you're asking, you're blurring.

## Explain It Back

Ask: *"In your own words — why a team of specialists instead of one do-it-all AI? And name
one teammate plus the job you'd give them."* Listen for the role-over-name idea. If they
fixate on the names, gently remind them the lineup changes; the roles don't.

## Scenario Check (light — predict, then reveal) — "Draft Your Crew"

Make it a quick game. Give 3 scenarios; for each, they pick **who they want on the team**
(one teammate, or "the whole room"), then reveal:

> 1. *"You're not even sure your idea is worth building."* → **Mary** (Analyst) to
>    pressure-test it before you commit.
> 2. *"Two parts of your app keep making clashing technical choices."* → **Winston**
>    (Architect) to make the decision explicit so everyone follows it.
> 3. *"A big launch decision with real tradeoffs, and opinions differ."* → **Party Mode** —
>    get the whole room debating it (Winston has a take, John weighs timeline, Sally flags
>    the user angle).

Reward the *reasoning*. If they pick a plausible alternative and defend it well, that's a
win — there's rarely one right hire.

## Apply to Your Build

Update the **M3 line** in `MY_BUILD.md`:

> *The teammate I'd most want on my build right now is ____, because ____ — and the first
> job I'd give them is ____.*

Draft a first version from their build details, then let them tweak it.
